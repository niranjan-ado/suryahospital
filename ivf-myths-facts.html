IVF Explained: Separating Facts from Fear

Infertility is a medical condition, not a life sentence. We clear up the confusion surrounding IVF treatment in India with evidence-based facts.

  • Success Rates

  • Multiple Births

  • Conclusion

In the last decade, In-Vitro Fertilisation (IVF) has transformed from a whispered topic into one of the most viable medical options for couples struggling to conceive. Yet, despite the science, misconceptions persist in many parts of India.

According to the World Health Organization (WHO), infertility affects nearly 1 in 6 people globally. It is a medical condition of the reproductive system, not a flaw in character. Unfortunately, families often rely on conflicting advice from friends or unverified social media sources, leading to unnecessary anxiety.

At Surya Hospital, we believe informed patients make the best decisions. Let us dismantle the five most common myths with clinical evidence.

block MYTH: "IVF is only a last resort."
The Medical Fact

In reality, infertility has diverse causes. While doctors often begin with simpler treatments like ovulation induction or IUI (Intrauterine Insemination), IVF may be the first-line recommendation for specific conditions such as:

  • Severe Tubal Blockage (where eggs cannot travel to the uterus).
  • Severe Male Factor Infertility (low sperm count/motility).
  • Advanced Maternal Age (where time is a critical factor).
  • Endometriosis.

Viewing IVF as a "last stage" often delays necessary treatment, reducing the chances of success as the couple ages.

Does IVF Guarantee Pregnancy?

A widespread assumption is that IVF is a "magic bullet" that guarantees pregnancy immediately. While IVF significantly increases conception chances compared to natural attempts (especially in sub-fertile couples), it is a biological process, not a transaction.

Success rates vary largely by the woman's age. For women under 35, success rates are high, but they naturally decline with age. Doctors design specific protocols—such as ICSI or Blastocyst Culture—to maximize these odds. The key is to have realistic expectations and view it as a journey that might take more than one cycle.

Are IVF Babies "Artificial"?

block MYTH: "IVF babies are weaker or prone to illness."
The Scientific Consensus

This is perhaps the most emotional myth. IVF babies are just as healthy as naturally conceived children. The term "In Vitro" simply means "in glass" (the petri dish), where fertilization happens. Once the embryo is transferred back to the uterus, the pregnancy progresses naturally.

In fact, with modern techniques like PGT (Pre-implantation Genetic Testing), specialists can screen embryos for chromosomal abnormalities before transfer, potentially reducing the risk of certain genetic diseases in the child.

The Truth About Twins & Triplets

Many people assume IVF inevitably leads to a twin or triplet pregnancy. Historically, clinics transferred multiple embryos to increase success rates, which did lead to multiple births.

However, global guidelines (including those from ASRM and ESHRE) now advocate for eSET (elective Single Embryo Transfer). Advances in laboratory technology allow us to identify the single strongest embryo for transfer. The goal of modern fertility treatment is a healthy singleton pregnancy, avoiding the risks associated with multiple births for both mother and baby.

Pain & Recovery

block MYTH: "It requires complete bed rest."
Lifestyle Fact

The process is far less intimidating than rumors suggest.

  • Injections: Modern hormonal injections are subcutaneous (like insulin pens), with very fine needles causing minimal discomfort.
  • Egg Retrieval: This is a short procedure done under sedation/anesthesia. You sleep through it and feel no pain.
  • Recovery: Most women resume their daily routine the next day. "Bed rest" does not improve success rates; in fact, gentle blood flow from normal activity is beneficial.

Conclusion

IVF is a medical procedure rooted in science, designed to bypass biological hurdles. If you have been trying to conceive for more than 12 months (or 6 months if over age 35), consulting a specialist is not an admission of defeat—it is a proactive step towards building your family.
